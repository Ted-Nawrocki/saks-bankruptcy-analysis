Executive Summary

Saks Global Enterprises LLC and 112 affiliates filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy protection on January 14, 2026, following an ill-timed acquisition, unsustainable capital structure, and severe deterioration in vendor relationships. This brief provides corporate actions professionals with comprehensive analysis of the capital structure, creditor composition, and key restructuring milestones.

The $136 million claim from Chanel is extraordinarily large for a trade creditor in retail bankruptcy. Understanding the August 2025 Liability Management Exercise (LME)
The creation of SPV Notes, 2O Notes, and 3O Notes in August 2025 represents a "Liability Management Exercise"—a debt restructuring technique that has become controversial in bankruptcy circles (sometimes called the "J.Crew Trap" after similar maneuvers). Saks Global effectively: (1) moved $2.2B of original acquisition debt into a Special Purpose Vehicle (SGUS LLC), (2) issued new notes with different priority rankings, and (3) raised $600M in new money at the cost of significantly impairing original bondholders who didn't participate in the exchange. S&P Global Ratings classified this as a "tantamount to default" because it resulted in a $115M discount to face value for non-consenting lenders. This maneuver bought Saks ~5 months before bankruptcy but fractured bondholder unity and may complicate the restructuring.

How Did We Get Here? Historical Context

A comprehensive timeline of strategic decisions, market dynamics, and operational failures that led to the January 2026 bankruptcy filing.

July 29, 2013
Hudson's Bay Company Acquires Saks Fifth Avenue
Richard Baker's HBC acquires Saks Inc. for $2.9 billion. Industry analysts note even at acquisition that Saks was struggling and "wouldn't be viable as a standalone business" long-term. Baker, a real estate executive, begins multi-brand consolidation strategy focused on extracting real estate value.
2015-2019
European Expansion Failures
Baker acquires Germany's Galeria Kaufhof (2015), expands into Netherlands with Hudson's Bay stores (2017). All European operations sold at losses and exited by 2019. Lord & Taylor sold for $100M (2019) and subsequently liquidated.
February 2020
HBC Goes Private Under Baker Control
Shareholders vote to take Hudson's Bay Company private in deal led by Richard Baker. Company begins focusing on Saks Fifth Avenue as core luxury asset while divesting non-core holdings.
December 2024
Neiman Marcus Acquisition Closes ($2.7B)
HBC completes acquisition of Neiman Marcus Group (including Bergdorf Goodman) for $2.7 billion, creating Saks Global. Deal financed with $2.2B in new bonds. Amazon invests $475M for e-commerce partnership. Apollo Global provides $1.5B in financing. Acquisition closes amid weakening luxury retail environment.
February 2025
Vendor Payment Crisis Acknowledged
CEO Marc Metrick publicly acknowledges backlog of overdue payments to vendors. Promises outstanding invoices will be paid beginning July 2025. Vendors begin withholding inventory, creating supply chain disruptions. Published credit reports indicate Days Beyond Terms significantly above industry norms.
March 2025
Hudson's Bay Company (Canada) Files for Creditor Protection
Parent company HBC files under Canadian CCAA, liquidating 74 stores and laying off 9,000 employees. Canadian operations including Saks locations begin wind-down after 355 years in business.
May 2025
Q1 Results: Revenue Down 14% Pro Forma
First quarter revenue declines 14% on pro forma basis despite 78% increase on reported basis (due to Neiman's acquisition). Company cites inventory shortages from vendor payment issues as primary cause. Vendor lawsuits begin accumulating (Jovani Fashion, Catherine Regehr, others).
August 2025
Emergency Debt Restructuring ("Tantamount to Default")
Saks Global completes liability management exercise: $600M new money raised, $2.2B senior secured notes exchanged for SPV notes ($762M), second-out notes ($1.4B), and third-out notes ($441M), all maturing 2029. S&P Global classifies as "tantamount to default" due to $115M discount to face value. Amazon does not consent to refinancing. S&P assigns CCC rating with negative outlook, citing "default risk in next 12 months without improvement."
October 2025
Q2 Results: Revenue Down 13% to $1.6B
Second quarter revenue falls 13% to $1.6B (from $2B+ prior year). Inventory challenges continue. Free operating cash flow deficit reaches $410M year-to-date. Published credit reports indicate vendor payment delinquencies surging significantly through late 2025. Bonds trading at 44% of face value.
November 2025
Store Closures & Asset Sales Begin
Announces closure of 9-10 Saks OFF 5TH locations. Holiday light show returns to Fifth Avenue flagship (had been cancelled in 2024 for cost savings). Traffic data shows foot traffic down 5.7% at Saks Fifth Avenue for full year 2025.
December 23, 2025
Beverly Hills Flagship Sale & Going Concern Warning
Sells 184,000 sq ft Neiman Marcus Beverly Hills flagship to Ashkenazy Acquisition Corp. for undisclosed amount to raise emergency cash. Company warns it may not be able to continue as "going concern" and that $100M debt payment due December 31 may not be made.
December 30, 2025
Missed ~$100M Interest Payment
Company misses approximately $100 million interest payment to bondholders due December 30, triggering technical default. Negotiations with creditors intensify around forbearance and DIP financing. Vendor payments completely deteriorate.
January 2, 2026
CEO Marc Metrick Departs
30-year Saks veteran steps down "to pursue new opportunities." Richard Baker briefly assumes dual role of Executive Chairman and CEO as bankruptcy filing becomes imminent.
January 14, 2026
Chapter 11 Bankruptcy Filing
Saks Global and 112 affiliates file for bankruptcy in Houston. Secures commitment for ~$1.75B financing package ($1B DIP + $500M exit + $240M ABL). Richard Baker steps down; Geoffroy van Raemdonck (who led Neiman Marcus through 2020 bankruptcy) takes over as CEO. Leadership team includes Darcy Penick (President/CCO), Lana Todorovich (Chief of Global Brand Partnerships), and Brandy Richardson (CFO).
January 15, 2026
Judge Approves DIP Despite Amazon Objection
Judge Alfredo R. Pérez approves $1.75B DIP financing after 7.5-hour hearing. Overrules Amazon's objection regarding Fifth Avenue flagship collateral pledge. Amazon reserves rights to pursue claims for alleged mismanagement and breach of LLC Agreement.
February 23, 2026
Key Bankruptcy Milestones Scheduled
Final hearing scheduled for 9:00 AM CT. Section 341 Meeting of Creditors scheduled for 1:00 PM CT (telephonic). Company targets emergence "later this year" (2026).
Sources: Bloomberg, Reuters, WSJ, The Robin Report (Robin Lewis retail analysis), Wikipedia (verified against primary sources), S&P Global Ratings, court filings

The Richard Baker Track Record: Pattern Analysis (2006-2026)

Comprehensive review of Richard Baker's retail stewardship reveals consistent pattern of value extraction over operational excellence:

  • 2006: NRDC acquires Lord & Taylor - real estate focused strategy from inception
  • 2008: Acquires Hudson's Bay Company following majority shareholder's death
  • 2013: HBC acquires Saks Fifth Avenue for $2.9B despite being "already struggling and wouldn't be viable standalone"
  • 2015: Acquires Germany's Galeria Kaufhof; later exited at loss (2019)
  • 2017: Disastrous Netherlands expansion; all stores closed by 2019
  • 2019: Sells Lord & Taylor for $100M (acquired 2006); chain liquidates shortly after
  • 2024: Engineers $2.7B Neiman Marcus acquisition saddled with $2.2B new debt during luxury downturn
  • 2025 (March): Canadian HBC files for protection, liquidating 74 stores, 9,000 layoffs after 355 years
  • 2026 (Jan 13): Saks Global bankruptcy; Baker exits after 2 weeks as CEO
Analytical Observation
Industry experts including Neil Saunders (GlobalData) and Robin Lewis (The Robin Report) characterize Baker's approach as "monetizing and squeezing" assets rather than building sustainable retail operations. His tenure saw collapse or divestiture of Lord & Taylor, European ventures, Canadian HBC, and now Saks Global—while successfully extracting real estate value throughout. Quote from analyst: "To be fair to Richard Baker... these businesses don't really have that much of a future, so it's kind of sensible to try and monetize and squeeze them as much as you can."
Vendor Payment Crisis: The Vicious Cycle Mechanics

Detailed timeline of vendor relations deterioration and feedback loop dynamics:

Phase 1 - Initial Stress (2023-Early 2024):
Vendors report late payments from Saks Fifth Avenue before Neiman acquisition. Days Beyond Terms begin trending above industry norms.

Phase 2 - Acquisition Impact (Dec 2024-Feb 2025):
Neiman deal closes. Combined entity immediately faces liquidity constraints. CEO acknowledges backlog in February 2025, promises resolution by July.

Phase 3 - Acceleration (May-August 2025):
Published credit reports indicate Days Beyond Terms significantly exceeding industry norms. CEO claims "largely in line" with payments; vendors report no improvement. Multiple lawsuits filed (Jovani, Catherine Regehr, International Trimmings, Criteo Corp, etc.)

Phase 4 - Cascade (Sept-Dec 2025):
Credit monitoring firms report escalating vendor payment delinquencies through late 2025. Vendors withhold inventory → bare shelves → customers shop competitors (Bloomingdale's reports strong 2025) → revenue down 11-14% → less cash for vendors → further withholding.
Critical Insight: Why $600M Wasn't Enough
The August 2025 refinancing raised $600M specifically to address vendor issues and restock inventory. Why did it fail? (1) Vendor trust already destroyed—many refused to ship even with new capital, (2) Debt service on $2.8B+ funded debt consumed cash flow, (3) Luxury market softening reduced sales velocity, (4) $600M insufficient to both pay down $337M+ critical vendor arrears AND maintain ongoing payments AND restock depleted inventory across the multi-brand portfolio. S&P Global noted the capital "was insufficient to fully reestablish inventory flow and achieve a meaningful operational turnaround."
Amazon Dispute: Full Analysis of Fifth Avenue Flagship Conflict

Detailed breakdown of Amazon's investment, objection, and implications:

Amazon's Original Investment Structure:
  • $475M equity investment at time of Neiman acquisition (2024)
  • $900M commercial agreement over 8 years to host Saks shop on Amazon platform
  • Security interest: "Equity cushion" in Fifth Avenue flagship entity (Saks Fifth Avenue HoldCo II)
  • Purpose: Power e-commerce infrastructure, technology integration
The Objection (January 15, 2026 Hearing):
Amazon claimed Saks Global:
1. Pledged Fifth Avenue flagship to secure DIP financing despite prior pledge to Amazon
2. Completed August 2025 refinancing without Amazon's consent (violation of LLC Agreement)
3. Engaged in "mismanagement, improper governance, and disregard of corporate separateness"
4. "Continuously failed to meet budgets, burned through hundreds of millions in less than a year"
5. Left Amazon's equity investment "presumptively worthless"
Amazon's Relief Requested:
  • One-day delay in DIP approval
  • Removal of Fifth Avenue flagship entity from DIP collateral package
  • Alternative: Amazon offered to provide separate DIP for HoldCo II entity
Court Ruling & Implications:
Judge Pérez overruled objection after 7.5-hour hearing, approved DIP as proposed. However, Amazon reserved all rights to pursue claims. This creates ongoing litigation risk that could: (1) complicate emergence, (2) trigger disputes over asset sales, (3) impact Fifth Avenue flagship disposition, (4) create claims for damages that prime unsecured creditors.
Strategic Implication
Amazon's reserved rights represent a "sword of Damocles" over the restructuring. If Amazon proves LLC Agreement violations, it could seek: (1) damages for dilution of security interest, (2) unwinding of August 2025 refinancing, (3) enhanced claims in the estate. For corporate actions professionals: monitor for Amazon adversary proceedings and any settlement discussions that could impact recovery waterfalls.
Market Context: Department Stores vs. Direct-to-Consumer Luxury

Structural analysis of luxury retail market dynamics:

Department Store Model Under Pressure:
Historical Role: Department stores traditionally served as "gatekeeper" to luxury brands—providing merchandising expertise, customer discovery, and geographic reach.

Current Reality: Brands increasingly view department stores as commoditized distribution channel that extracts margin without commensurate value-add. Direct-to-consumer offers higher margins, better customer data, and brand control.

Evidence of Shift:
• LVMH, Hermès, Chanel expanding owned retail footprint
• Macy's closed hundreds of stores (2024)
• Lord & Taylor liquidated (2020)
• Widespread department store closures continuing through 2025
But Luxury Market Itself Performed Differently:
  • LVMH (world's largest luxury group): +1% growth Q3 2024
  • Bloomingdale's: Strong 2025 sales (benefited from Saks' weakness)
  • Analyst quote: "Rich people are still buying, just not so much at Saks" (David Swartz, Morningstar)
Saks-Specific vs. Sector-Wide:
Dana Telsey (Telsey Advisory): "This is a company specific incident that occurred." The debt-fueled merger at peak of leverage cycle, during luxury softening, with vendor trust already broken, created perfect storm. Market context exacerbated but didn't cause the bankruptcy—execution did.
